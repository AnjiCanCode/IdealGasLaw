--- title: "Intermolecular Attraction" ---

Real Particles Attract Each Other

The second way real gases differ from ideal: molecules attract each other through van der Waals forces.

Watch the particles on the right; they tend to cluster slightly. They're not completely independent like ideal gas particles.

Use the slider below to change the attraction strength.

Crank the attraction to maximum.

Strong attraction causes the particles to clump together. The gas starts to condense!

Notice the pressure on the graph will gradually decline (after a potential initial spike). Why? Because particles that are being pulled back by their neighbors hit the walls with less force.

Now turn attraction to zero.

Without attraction, the particles spread out and the pressure rises, back to ideal gas behavior!

The van der Waals equation corrects for this with the a parameter:

Peffective = Pmeasured + a(n/V)²

At high temperature, particles move too fast for attractions to matter. At low temperature, attractions dominate and can even cause liquefaction.
