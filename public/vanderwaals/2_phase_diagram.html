Beyond Ideal Interactive Gas Laws

Phase Transitions

The van der Waals equation predicts something the ideal gas law cannot: phase transitions.

The particles on the right start as a solid — locked in a crystal lattice, barely jiggling.

Slowly increase the temperature.

The particles jiggle in place — they're a solid.

Keep increasing.

The particles start "walking" around each other, losing their rigid structure. The solid is melting into a liquid.

Keep going!

Particles start breaking free from the group entirely — the liquid is evaporating into a gas.

At high enough temperature, the particles bounce freely — attractions can't hold them together anymore. This is a gas.

The ideal gas law only describes this last state. The van der Waals equation bridges the gap, explaining why real gases can condense when cooled and why there's a critical temperature above which a gas cannot be liquefied no matter the pressure.
