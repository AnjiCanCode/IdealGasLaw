Non-Ideal Mixtures

Real molecules interact differently depending on what they're near. A-A interactions might differ from A-B interactions.

Right now, all interactions are equal, the particles mix freely. Use the slider to change how A and B interact with each other.

Try maximizing the A-B attraction.

Strong cross-species attraction causes A and B to cluster together. The mixture behaves very differently from a simple sum of its parts. This is a negative deviation from Raoult's Law, the effective pressure is lower than the ideal mixture prediction.

Now reduce A-B interaction to near zero (repulsive only).

Without cross-attraction, the A and B particles separate, like-attracts-like wins. Each species forms its own cluster. This is phase separation, the same phenomenon behind oil and water.

Real chemical mixtures, solutions of ethanol and water, mixtures of industrial gases, behave this way. The ideal gas law and Dalton's law assume no interactions, but real chemistry is all about interactions.
